What is an LLM?

If you have used ChatGPT, Copilot, or Claude, you have used a Large Language Model. Here is the picture underneath.

An LLM is the brain that learned the patterns of language. It writes; it does not act.

Chapter 1 · Foundations
accelerProprietary and confidential

But on its own, an LLM cannot do these.

Three honest limits. Each one is a reason agents exist.

⏱

See today's data

Trained on text up to a point in the past. Does not know what happened this week.

Ask: "What's the weather in Seattle today?"
It cannot tell you.
⚡

Take any action

It can describe what to do. It cannot actually search, send, or post on its own.

Ask: "Email this brief to my manager."
It will write the email, not send it.
🏢

Know your world

Does not know your products, your customers, your CRM, or your internal docs.

Ask: "Summarize last week's pipeline."
It does not know your pipeline.

An agent closes all three gaps. It plans, uses tools, and takes action in your systems — not just answers, outcomes.

First, the agent needs to know what its job is.

Without this, the LLM has no idea what kind of output you want. So step one: tell it.

Piece 1 of 4
The agent needs a clear, written description of its job, what to do, what NOT to do, what the output looks like.
📋INSTRUCTIONS

Think of it as a job description, written in plain English. The agent reads it on every request before doing anything.

It's the highest-leverage thing in any agent. A vague Instruction = vague output. A precise one = sharp output.

Without it → the agent guesses, drifts off-topic, or refuses to help.

How Instructions flow through the agent

Next, the agent needs sources to draw from.

Instructions told it WHAT to do. Now we tell it WHERE to look for the facts.

Piece 2 of 4
The agent needs places to fetch information beyond what the LLM already learned during training.
📚KNOWLEDGE

The LLM only knows what it was trained on, old data, generic facts. Knowledge sources connect the agent to fresh information and your specific world.

Sources can be: web search (live), uploaded documents, internal sites (SharePoint), or even a database.

Without it → the brief reads like generic desk research from two years ago, with no specifics.

Then, the agent needs to take action.

Knowledge lets it read. Tools let it actually do things in the real world.

Piece 3 of 4
The agent needs ways to take real-world actions, search, send, post, update, not just generate text.
⚡TOOLS

A tool is anything the agent can do outside of just chatting. The agent decides when to use each tool, based on the request.

Common tools: web search, send email, look up calendar, post to chat, update CRM, query a database.

Without it → the agent is just a smarter chatbot, it talks, but nothing actually happens.

Tools, extended, Model Context Protocol (MCP).

Web Search is one tool. MCP is the universal standard that lets your agent connect to any external system, your CRM, your internal docs, anything that speaks MCP.

Today your agent uses Web Search. With MCP, you can connect Salesforce, LinkedIn Sales Nav, internal docs, or anything else, in minutes instead of weeks.

Chapter 3 · Anatomy, decoded · Tools 2/2, MCP
accelerProprietary and confidential

Finally, the agent needs to know when to wake up.

An agent sitting idle isn't useful. We need to define what kicks it into action.

Piece 4 of 4
The agent needs a defined event that causes it to start working.
🔔TRIGGERS

A trigger is whatever causes the agent to run. The simplest one is "you message it." More advanced: a calendar event, a CRM stage change, or a scheduled time.

Same agent can have different triggers over time, start simple, automate later.

Without it → the agent never runs. It just sits there, waiting.

    Trust & Guardrails
    The rules that keep agents reliable.
    Chapter map
    accelerProprietary and confidential

    LLMs are brilliant, and occasionally make things up.

    It's called hallucination. A known property of how LLMs work, they're trained to sound right, even when they don't actually know. Here's what it looks like:

    WITHOUT GUARDRAILS

    An ungrounded brief

    Talking point: "Their new CHRO mentioned at TechCrunch Disrupt 2026 that they're investing $50M in AI literacy training this year."

    ✗ Plausible-sounding. Specific numbers. Cited a real event.
    ✗ None of it is verifiable. The LLM invented it.

    → You walk into the meeting and quote it. The customer says "we never said that."

    WITH GUARDRAILS

    A grounded brief

    Talking point: "Their CHRO announced an expanded leadership development program in their Q1 earnings call." [source: investor.example.com/q1-2026]

    ✓ Every claim has a source URL.
    ✓ If the agent can't find a source, the claim is left out.

    → You walk in with verifiable signals you can hand the customer.

    Chapter 5 · Trust & Guardrails
    accelerProprietary and confidential

    Guardrails.

    Plain-English rules in the Instructions that keep the agent honest, focused, and inside its lane.

    ❓
    What would stop the agent from inventing facts that sound true?
    click to flip ↻
    Cite every source.
    In the Instructions: "every claim in the brief must include a source URL."
    If the agent did not find a source, it cannot make the claim. No invented news.
    ❓
    What would stop the agent from guessing when your request is ambiguous?
    click to flip ↻
    When unsure, ask.
    In the Instructions: "if a company name is ambiguous, ask the user."
    "Prep for Apple" gets back "Apple Inc., Apple Bank, or Apple Records?" instead of a wrong guess.
    ❓
    What would stop the agent from drifting into work that is not its job?
    click to flip ↻
    Stay in scope.
    In the Instructions: "only generate pre-call briefs. For anything else, politely redirect."
    "Draft me a full sales email" gets back "That is outside my scope. I prepare pre-call briefs only."

    These are three common ones. Add as many guardrails as your use case needs. They all live in the Instructions.
